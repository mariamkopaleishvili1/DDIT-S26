## Overview

The Case Study Analysis requires you to conduct a comprehensive strategic analysis of a major transformation case using the frameworks developed across the course. This is an individual assignment. The goal is not to summarise what happened. The goal is to apply structured analytical frameworks to produce an original strategic assessment and a defensible set of alternative recommendations.

A strong analysis does not describe the case. It diagnoses, evaluates, and recommends with evidence and logic.

## Required Scope

Your analysis must address all five of the following sections. Allocate page length proportionally to analytical weight, not to the order listed.

### Section 1. Industry and Competitive Context (approximately 2 pages)

Analyse the industry at the time the disruption began. Do not describe the industry in generic terms. Use frameworks to structure your analysis.

Required elements:
- Porter's Five Forces analysis applied to the industry at the relevant time
- Value chain analysis identifying where disruption pressure was entering
- Identification of the main incumbents, their strategic positions, and their primary vulnerabilities
- Customer context: what were customers doing, expecting, and tolerating before the disruption?

Avoid: listing facts about the company. Analyse the structural conditions of the industry.

---

### Section 2. Disruption Forces and Strategic Implications (approximately 2 pages)

Apply Christensen's framework and Rogers' five-domain model to diagnose the nature of the disruption.

Required elements:
- Classification of the disruption: low-end, new-market, sustaining, or architectural. Justify your classification with evidence.
- Apply Rogers' five domains: which domains were most affected? How did customer behaviour, competitive dynamics, data strategy, innovation logic, and value capture all shift?
- Apply Yoo et al.'s digital innovation architecture: which layers of the digital stack changed and why did that matter strategically?
- Identify the strategic implications for incumbents at the point the disruption was visible but not yet dominant

The classification must be argued, not asserted. If the case does not fit Christensen cleanly, say so and explain why. That is a stronger answer than forcing a fit.

---

### Section 3. Critical Evaluation of the Transformation Approach (approximately 3 pages)

This is the analytical centrepiece of the paper. Evaluate what the focal company or incumbent actually did in response to the disruption.

Required elements:
- What strategic choices did the company make and why did those choices make sense at the time?
- What did the company get right? Support claims with evidence.
- What did the company get wrong or too late? Support claims with evidence.
- Evaluate the operating model transformation: did the organisation adapt its capabilities, governance, and culture at the pace required?
- Evaluate the financial logic: did the transformation investments produce measurable returns? If data is available, include it.
- Identify the single most important decision point in the case and explain why it was pivotal.

Avoid: using hindsight to criticise obvious failures without explaining why the failure was not obvious at the time. Strong analysis understands the conditions that produced the decision, not just the outcome.

---

### Section 4. Application of Course Frameworks (approximately 2 pages)

Apply at least three specific course frameworks beyond those already used in Sections 1 and 2. Select frameworks that illuminate something the simpler analysis missed.

Eligible frameworks include:
- Three Horizons of Innovation
- Platform strategy and network effects
- AI transformation maturity model
- Jobs-to-be-done competition analysis
- Pyramid Principle applied to evaluate the company's communication to stakeholders
- Hypothesis-driven problem-solving applied to the company's strategic process

For each framework applied, state what new insight it produces. Do not apply frameworks mechanically. Apply them because they reveal something.

---

### Section 5. Alternative Recommendations (approximately 2 pages)

Propose two alternative strategic paths that the company could have taken at the critical decision point you identified in Section 3.

For each alternative:
- State the recommendation precisely: what should the company have done, when, and why?
- Explain the logic: which evidence and framework reasoning supports this recommendation?
- Acknowledge the tradeoffs: what would this recommendation have required the company to give up?
- Assess feasibility: was this recommendation actually achievable given the company's constraints at the time?

Recommendations without tradeoffs are not recommendations. They are wishes. Strong recommendations acknowledge what they cost.

## Common Failure Modes

These are the most frequent reasons papers score below 14 out of 20:

**Summarising rather than analysing.** A well-written Wikipedia summary is not an analysis. Every paragraph should be making an analytical claim, not reporting an event.

**Using frameworks as labels.** Naming a framework in a heading and then describing the case without applying the framework is not framework application. The framework must change what you see.

**Forcing Christensen.** Not every digital challenge is disruptive innovation by Christensen's definition. If the case does not fit, argue why not. That demonstrates stronger understanding than forcing a fit.

**Recommendations without feasibility.** "The company should have adopted a platform strategy" is not a recommendation. State what specifically, when, what it would have cost, and what would have had to be given up.

**Missing the critical decision point.** Strong analyses identify the specific moment when a different choice could have changed the outcome. Vague claims about culture or leadership are not decision points.
